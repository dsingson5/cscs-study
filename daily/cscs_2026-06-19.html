Motor units & the size principle

A motor unit = one motor neuron + all the fibers it innervates; those fibers are metabolically alike, so a unit is slow-twitch (Type I), fast-twitch/fatigue-resistant (IIa), or fast-twitch/fatigable (IIx). By the all-or-none principle, firing a motor neuron contracts every fiber in its unit. Muscle grades force two ways: recruitment (number of active units) and rate coding (firing frequency). The size principle orders recruitment small→large — low-threshold Type I first, high-threshold Type II only as demand rises — so heavier or more explosive efforts pull in the fast units.

Key facts to memorize
  • Motor unit = 1 motor neuron + fibers it innervates; all same type
  • All-or-none: fire the neuron → every fiber in that unit contracts
  • Force graded by recruitment (# units) + rate coding (firing frequency)
  • Size principle: low-threshold Type I → higher-threshold IIa → IIx as demand ↑
  • Weighted curl recruits BOTH low-threshold slow + high-threshold fast units (unweighted = low only)
  • Small muscles (eye/hand) mostly rely on rate coding; large (thigh) also need heavy recruitment
  • Maximal high-intensity weightlifting EMG ≈ ≥90% of available MUs activated
  • Twitch summation → unfused → fused tetanus = max tetanic force of a unit
  • IIx: largest neuron, highest threshold, fastest, most force/RFD, least fatigue-resistant
  • Full motor-neuron-pool activation likely NOT achievable in untrained people
Visual glossary
Motor unit
One motor neuron plus all muscle fibers it innervates. Every fiber in the unit is the same type and contracts together.
Size Principle
Motor units are recruited smallest to largest: low-threshold Type I first, higher-threshold Type IIa then IIx as force demand rises.
Rate coding
Grading force by the firing frequency of a motor neuron; faster firing makes twitches summate into greater, then tetanic, force.
Recruitment
Increasing force by activating additional motor units; dominant force mechanism in large muscles.
All-or-none principle
An action potential in a motor neuron activates every fiber of its unit, or none — there is no partial activation of a single unit.
Tetanus
Fusion of overlapping twitches at high firing frequency into a sustained maximal force; unfused (partial) then fused (smooth).
Recruitment threshold
The force/effort level at which a given motor unit turns on. Low for Type I, high for Type II.

Zone-2 bike work sits far below threshold and only cycles low-threshold Type I units, so it can't substitute for heavy or maximal-intent lifting. Endocrine II — Catabolic hormones & stress

Two adrenal stress systems drive the catabolic side of the endocrine response. The cortex, cued by ACTH via the HPA axis, secretes cortisol (chief glucocorticoid): it spares glucose, fuels gluconeogenesis, and mobilizes fat — adaptive acutely. If it fails to return to baseline within ~24 h it turns catabolic/anti-anabolic: ↓protein synthesis, ↑protein breakdown, blunted anabolic signaling, immune suppression. The medulla fires catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine) via sympathetic drive — the fight-or-flight arm: ↑force, ↑contraction rate, ↑HR/BP, glycogenolysis, and it primes testosterone release.

Key facts to memorize
  • Adrenal cortex → cortisol (glucocorticoid), driven by ACTH via HPA axis
  • Adrenal medulla → catecholamines (epi, norepi, dopamine) via direct sympathetic stimulation
  • Cortisol acute = adaptive (glucose sparing, gluconeogenesis, fat oxidation); chronic >24h = catabolic/anti-anabolic
  • Chronic cortisol ↓protein synthesis, ↑protein degradation, suppresses immune function
  • Cortisol ↑ with high-volume, large-muscle, short-rest resistance work
  • Trained athletes convert cortisol→cortisone more efficiently (protective adaptation)
  • Catecholamines ↑ force, contraction rate, BP, blood flow (vasodilation); prime testosterone secretion
  • Epinephrine correlates with blood lactate (metabolic stress marker)
  • Overtraining → adrenal exhaustion (↓catecholamine output) + β2-receptor downregulation → ↓contractile force
Visual glossary
Cortisol
Primary glucocorticoid from the adrenal cortex; raises glucose availability under stress but is catabolic and anti-anabolic when chronically elevated.
Catecholamines
Epinephrine, norepinephrine, and dopamine from the adrenal medulla; the fight-or-flight hormones that boost force, contraction speed, and cardiovascular output.
HPA axis
Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal pathway; hypothalamus → ACTH from anterior pituitary → cortisol from adrenal cortex, the systemic regulator of the stress response.
Gluconeogenesis
Synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate sources (amino acids, glycerol); a cortisol-driven route to spare/supply glucose during prolonged stress.
Adrenal exhaustion
Reduced medullary capacity to secrete catecholamines effectively after continuous high stress without varied training or rest.
Receptor downregulation
Loss of receptor number or affinity from chronic overstimulation; e.g., overtraining downregulates β2-receptors, blunting epinephrine's effect on force.

1 week backExercise ScienceCh 2 (5E) · 5E Ch 2 — Muscle Length, Joint Angle, Muscle Contraction Velocity, Levers of the Musculoskeletal System

Biomechanics II — Force-velocity & length-tension

Muscle force depends on length, velocity, and leverage. Peak force sits near resting length, where actin–myosin overlap gives max potential crossbridges; too stretched → fewer aligned sites, too short → excessive overlap, both ↓ force. As concentric velocity ↑, force ↓ (steepest drop at slow speeds, per A.V. Hill), so eccentric > isometric > concentric. Because movement is rotation, muscle force acts as torque (force × moment arm). Most limb muscles work at a mechanical disadvantage (MA < 1.0), so internal muscle forces far exceed the external load.

Heavy slow lifts — high force, low velocity, low power. Strength training territory.
Drag the dot along the curve. Peak power lives at ~30% max isometric force — the F-V intersection.
⚖️Torque & moment arm — find the sticking point
Resistance torque on the elbow: low  |  moment arm = short
Before you touch the slider — at which elbow angle is the dumbbell's resistance torque the GREATEST?
Greatest at ~90° (forearm horizontal). Torque = force × perpendicular moment arm. Gravity always pulls straight down, so the moment arm is the horizontal distance from the elbow to the weight — longest when the forearm is horizontal, near-zero when it points straight down or straight up. Why it matters: that horizontal point is your sticking point — where the resistance curve peaks. Drag the slider to watch the moment arm (blue) and torque grow to a max mid-rep, then fall off. It's also why accommodating resistance (bands/chains) and exercise selection are built around where torque demand is highest.
Key facts to memorize
  • Force order: eccentric > isometric > concentric
  • F–V (Hill): concentric torque ↓ as angular velocity ↑; decline steepest at slow speeds (non-linear)
  • Eccentric torque ↑ with velocity up to ~90°/s (~1.57 rad/s), then gradual ↓
  • Length–tension peak = resting length (max actin–myosin overlap); too long or too short ↓ force
  • Titin: mechanosensing 3rd filament; tightens with Ca²⁺ in eccentrics → aids force + hypertrophy signaling
  • Torque = force × moment arm; MA = muscle moment arm ÷ resistive moment arm
  • MA > 1.0 → muscle force < load; MA < 1.0 → muscle force > load
  • Levers: 1st-class = forces on opposite sides of fulcrum; 2nd = same side, MM > MR (advantage); 3rd = same side, MM < MR (disadvantage, most limb muscles)
  • Free-weight resistive moment arm = horizontal distance from load to joint → resistive torque varies through ROM (sticking points)
Visual glossary
Force-velocity curve
Force ↓ as concentric velocity ↑; eccentric force rises with speed to ~90°/s then plateaus. Order: eccentric > isometric > concentric.
Length-tension relationship
Force is maximal at resting length (optimal actin–myosin overlap); falls when the muscle is stretched (fewer aligned sites) or overly shortened (excessive overlap).
Moment arm
Perpendicular distance from a force's line of action to the fulcrum. Longer moment arm → more torque per unit of force.
Torque
Rotational effect of a force about a joint = force × moment arm. Muscle force must be expressed as torque because movement is joint rotation.
Mechanical advantage
Ratio of muscle moment arm to resistive moment arm. >1.0 lets muscle force be less than the load; <1.0 forces it to exceed the load.
Titin
Elastic third filament with mechanosensing properties; tightens under eccentric load and calcium influx, contributing to force and hypertrophy signaling.
Muscle action
Preferred over 'contraction': concentric (shortening, force > load), eccentric (lengthening, force < load), isometric (no length change, force = load).

Connection to your training

Your PM heavy squats/deadlifts train the high-force, low-velocity left side of the F–V curve; sled pushes and box jumps develop the right side. Program eccentric-emphasis lowers to exploit that eccentric force ceiling and drive titin-linked adaptation. For Hyrox, recognize that a wall-ball or lunge sticking point is a moment-arm problem — the resistive torque peaks mid-range — so cue athletes to accelerate hardest where leverage is worst.

2 weeks backProgram Design & TestingCh 18 (5E) · 5E Ch 18 — Step 5: Training Load & Repetitions; Step 6: Volume

Resistance program — intensity & volume

Load and reps are inversely related: heavier load → fewer reps. Set intensity as %1RM or as an xRM, then pick reps to hit the goal. RM continuum: strength ≤6 reps at ≥85%; power (multi-effort) 3-5 at 75-85%; hypertrophy 8-12 at 67-80% (but a wide load span, even <60% to failure, grows muscle); endurance ≥12 at ≤67%. Assign 1RM by direct test, estimate from an xRM, or test the goal-rep xRM directly. Volume = sets × reps; volume-load = sets × reps × load (a work proxy). Multiple sets beat single for trained lifters; progress via the 2-for-2 rule.

Key facts to memorize
  • %1RM→reps (Tbl 18.8): 100%=1, 95%=2, 90%=4, 85%=6, 80%=8, 75%=10, 67%=12, 65%=15
  • Load/rep by goal (Tbl 18.11): strength ≥85%/≤6; power multi-effort 75-85%/3-5; hypertrophy 67-80%/8-12; endurance ≤67%/≥12
  • 1RM assignment: direct 1RM test → estimate from xRM (e.g. 10RM) → or test the goal-rep xRM directly
  • Assistance exercises capped at ≤8RM load, even inside a heavy strength block
  • Volume-load = sets × reps × load (proxy for mechanical work); rep-volume = total reps
  • Hypertrophy: light loads (<60% to failure) ≈ heavy loads for growth; heavy is more time-efficient + adds strength
  • Volume (Tbl 18.13): trained strength 2-3 sets; hypertrophy 3-6 sets; multiple sets > single for intermediate/advanced
  • 2-for-2 rule: +load when athlete beats goal reps by ≥2 on the last set for 2 consecutive sessions
  • Load increase ~2.5-10% (upper body smaller, lower body larger); avoid habitual training to failure
Visual glossary
1RM
One-rep max — most weight liftable once with proper technique. Intensity is often prescribed as a % of it.
xRM
The most weight liftable for exactly x reps to true failure (e.g. a 10RM). Used to assign loads or estimate 1RM without maximal testing.
RM continuum
Load-vs-goal map: low reps/heavy → strength, mid → hypertrophy, high reps/light → endurance. Effects blend at any given RM.
Volume-load
Sets × reps × load — a proxy for mechanical work done in a session. Distinct from rep-volume (total reps).
2-for-2 rule
Progression cue: if the athlete exceeds the goal reps by 2+ on the last set in 2 consecutive workouts, add load next session.
RIR / RPE
Reps-in-reserve and rating of perceived exertion — subjective, autoregulatory intensity gauges best paired with %1RM. Prescribe as ranges (RIR 2-3, RPE 7-8).
Hypertrophy rep range
~8-12 reps at 67-80% 1RM classically, but growth spans a wide load range (even <60% to failure); mechanical tension is the driver.

Connection to your training

Your strength blocks should live in the ≤6 rep / ≥85% zone — 5x5, 4x4 on squat, deadlift, press — high stimulus, low rep-volume so it stacks cleanly on Hyrox conditioning. Keep assistance work capped at ~8RM to spare joints, and use RIR (leave 1-2 in reserve) instead of grinding to failure so next-day run and erg quality survive. Progress load by the 2-for-2 rule, not by chasing a rep PR every session.
