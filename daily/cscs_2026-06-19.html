Resistance program — intensity & volume

%1RM and rep ranges: strength 1-6 reps at 85-100%; hypertrophy 6-12 at 65-85%; muscular endurance 12+ at <65%; power 1-5 explosive at varied intensities (30-50% for ballistic, 75-90% for cleans). Volume = sets × reps × load. Weekly hard-set landmark (Schoenfeld): MV (maintenance) ≈ 6-10, MEV ≈ 10, MAV ≈ 12-20, MRV ≈ 20+ per muscle/wk for hypertrophy. Strength MRV lower.

Key facts to memorize
  • Strength: 1-6 reps, 85-100% 1RM, 3-5 sets, long rest
  • Hypertrophy: 6-12 reps, 65-85%, 3-6 sets
  • Endurance: 12+ reps, <65%, 2-3 sets
  • Power: 1-5 reps explosive, varied %
  • Volume landmarks: MV/MEV/MAV/MRV
Connection to your training

Your strength PM sessions should sit in low-rep, moderate-set range (5×5, 4×6 patterns) — high stimulus, low recovery cost stacked on top of your conditioning load.

Resistance program — exercise selection

Core exercises (multi-joint, large muscle): squat, deadlift, bench, OHP, row. Assistance (single-joint or smaller muscle). Structural exercises load the spine axially; power exercises are explosive (cleans, jumps). Sport-specific exercises mimic competitive movement patterns. Balance pushing/pulling, anterior/posterior, knee/hip dominance. Exercise order: power → core → assistance (most fatiguing → least).

Key facts to memorize
  • Core: multi-joint, large muscle group, structural
  • Order: power → core → assistance
  • Balance push/pull, ant/post, knee/hip
  • Sport-specific should resemble actual movement
  • Single-joint exercises target weak links
Connection to your training

Your sled push + KB swing + hinge variations are core/power; accessory single-joint lifts fill gaps.

Alternative modes — kettlebell, sled, strongman

KB swing: hip-hinge driven; bell floats with hip extension; not a squat. Goblet squat: front-loaded squat — good teaching tool. Turkish get-up: full-body integration. Sled push/drag: low eccentric load, high metabolic. Strongman (yoke, farmers, sandbag): odd-object, irregular loading; high-stability demand. Each has place in athletic training. Selection: match demand profile to sport.

Key facts to memorize
  • KB swing: hip hinge, NOT a squat
  • Sled: low eccentric → low DOMS, can do daily
  • Farmers: grip, posture, glute endurance
  • Yoke walk: spinal compression demand
  • Odd objects build trunk stability
Connection to your training

Sled push is highly Hyrox-specific. Low eccentric load means recovery cost is lower than the metabolic stress would suggest — useful as substitute for higher-impact intervals.

Resistance technique — squat

Back squat: bar on upper traps (high bar) or rear delts (low bar). Stance ~shoulder width, toes slight out. Cues: brace core (Valsalva for max effort), break at hips and knees together, descend keeping chest up, knees track over toes (not collapsed in). Depth ≥ parallel (femur). Drive through midfoot, hips and shoulders rise together. Spotters at sides for max attempts. Common faults: knee valgus, butt wink at bottom, forward lean.

Key facts to memorize
  • Bar position changes torque distribution between hips/knees
  • Parallel = femur parallel to floor
  • Valsalva increases intra-abdominal pressure → spine stability
  • Knee valgus is the most common fault; cue 'knees out'
  • Spotters at sides, never directly behind, for max attempts
Connection to your training

Your strength PM session structure relies on squat fidelity — these technique points are non-negotiable for your hip-dominant Hyrox prep.

Basic nutrition — macronutrients

Carbohydrate: 4 kcal/g; spares protein, primary fuel for higher-intensity work; stored as glycogen (~400 g muscle + 100 g liver). Recommendation for athletes: 5-10 g/kg/day depending on training. Protein: 4 kcal/g; structural and enzymatic. Athletes: 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day; up to 2.2 in hypertrophy phases. Fat: 9 kcal/g; essential for hormones, vitamin absorption. Minimum 20-30% kcal from fat. Hydration: ~30-40 mL/kg/day baseline + sweat replacement.

Key facts to memorize
  • CHO: 4 kcal/g; muscle glycogen ~400 g
  • PRO: 4 kcal/g; athlete intake 1.4-2.0 g/kg
  • FAT: 9 kcal/g; ≥20% of kcal
  • Hydration: 30-40 mL/kg + sweat
  • EAA leucine threshold ~2.5-3 g per meal for MPS
Connection to your training

Your training volume (Z2 + intervals + strength + Hyrox) puts CHO needs at high end (~6-8 g/kg). Underfueling chronically blunts everything from glycogen recovery to GH pulse to immune function.

Motor units & the size principle

A motor unit = one alpha motoneuron + all the fibers it innervates. All fibers in a single motor unit are the same type. The Size Principle (Henneman, 1965): motor units are recruited in order of size — smallest (Type I) first, largest (Type IIx) last, as force demand rises. Rate coding (firing frequency) and recruitment together grade force. Heavier loads or maximal-effort actions recruit higher-threshold units; this is why high-intensity work is non-negotiable for Type II development.

Key facts to memorize
  • Henneman's Size Principle — small to large recruitment order
  • Force gradation = recruitment + rate coding
  • All fibers in a motor unit are SAME type
  • High-threshold MUs recruited only at ~80%+ of max force OR at maximal velocities even with lighter loads
  • Doublet firing (2 rapid impulses) enhances rate of force development
Connection to your training

Your strength PM sessions need true high-intensity exposure to recruit Type IIx motor units. Z2 cycling never gets there — that's why bike day's strength block is structurally non-substitutable.
