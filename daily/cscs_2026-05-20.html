Motor units & the size principle

A motor unit = one alpha motoneuron + all the fibers it innervates. All fibers in a single motor unit are the same type. The Size Principle (Henneman, 1965): motor units are recruited in order of size — smallest (Type I) first, largest (Type IIx) last, as force demand rises. Rate coding (firing frequency) and recruitment together grade force. Heavier loads or maximal-effort actions recruit higher-threshold units; this is why high-intensity work is non-negotiable for Type II development.

Key facts to memorize
  • Henneman's Size Principle — small to large recruitment order
  • Force gradation = recruitment + rate coding
  • All fibers in a motor unit are SAME type
  • High-threshold MUs recruited only at ~80%+ of max force OR at maximal velocities even with lighter loads
  • Doublet firing (2 rapid impulses) enhances rate of force development
Visual glossary
Motor unit
One alpha motoneuron plus every fiber it innervates. All fibers in a unit are the same type.
Size Principle
Smallest (Type I) motor units fire first. Larger (Type IIa, then IIx) units recruit only as force demand rises.
Rate coding
After all units are recruited, force keeps rising via higher firing frequency of motoneurons.
Connection to your training

Your strength PM sessions need true high-intensity exposure to recruit Type IIx motor units. Z2 cycling never gets there — that's why bike day's strength block is structurally non-substitutable.

Skeletal muscle architecture & fiber types

Skeletal muscle is organized hierarchically: muscle → fasciculus → muscle fiber (one cell) → myofibril → sarcomere. The sarcomere (Z-disc to Z-disc) is the contractile unit; sliding-filament theory says actin slides along myosin via cross-bridge cycling, powered by ATP. Fibers are classified by myosin heavy chain isoform: Type I (slow oxidative, fatigue-resistant, low force, red), Type IIa (fast oxidative-glycolytic, intermediate), Type IIx (fast glycolytic, highest force, fatigues fast). Training shifts IIx → IIa; fiber-type proportion is largely genetic but trainable in expression.

Key facts to memorize
  • Sarcomere = Z-disc to Z-disc
  • Cross-bridge cycle requires ATP for both attachment AND detachment
  • Type I: high mitochondrial density, capillaries, myoglobin
  • Type IIx: largest cross-sectional area, highest force/velocity, lowest endurance
  • Heavy resistance training shifts IIx → IIa (not Type II → Type I)
Visual glossary
Sarcomere
The contractile unit, Z-disc to Z-disc. When muscle contracts, sarcomeres shorten — the thin filaments slide inward past the thick filament.
Myosin
Thick filament (blue). Its 'heads' grab actin and pull — the cross-bridge cycle.
Actin
Thin filament (green). Anchored at Z-discs, projects toward myosin. Has binding sites for myosin heads.
Type I (slow oxidative)
Red, mitochondria-rich, fatigue-resistant. Marathon fibers.
Type IIx (fast glycolytic)
Pale, glycogen-rich, highest force + speed but fatigues fast. Sprint fibers.
Connection to your training

Your aerobic base (HR 132 indoor Z2 cap, LT1=147) reflects Type I oxidative capacity. Your 4×8 sessions (HR 170-175 target) recruit Type IIa heavily — sub-MLSS endurance work develops the IIa pool you need for Hyrox station blocks.

Yes. The Size Principle is sensitive to intent and velocity, not just load. Maximal-effort movements at light loads (e.g., dynamic-effort method) can recruit high-threshold units.
