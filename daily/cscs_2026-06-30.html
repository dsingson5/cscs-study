Speed & agility training

Sprint mechanics: acceleration (0-20m, forward lean, long push), max velocity (upright, cyclic, vertical force production), deceleration. Coaching cues: arm drive, knee drive, dorsiflexion. Agility = ability to react and change direction; CODS = closed-loop change of direction. Reactive agility involves stimulus (visual/auditory). Programming: short distances at full intensity, full recovery (work:rest 1:12-1:20 for max speed).

▶Sprint mechanics — start to cut
Step 1 of 4
Acceleration is a forward-lean push; max velocity is tall with brief, straight-down ground force; agility is a controlled sink-and-plant to redirect.
Key facts to memorize
  • Acceleration: forward lean, long ground contact
  • Max velocity: upright, vertical force, brief contact
  • Work:rest for max-speed = 1:12 to 1:20
  • Reactive agility > closed-loop for transfer to sport
  • Sprint training is a power-skill — not a conditioning tool
Connection to your training

Hyrox running is sub-maximal pacing — true speed work won't transfer much. Skip dedicated speed unless you're chasing a 5K PR.

Endocrine II — Catabolic hormones & stress

Cortisol (adrenal cortex): primary glucocorticoid. Released in response to stress, including exercise. Elevates blood glucose (gluconeogenesis), mobilizes fat, BUT chronically elevated impairs protein synthesis and immune function. T:C ratio used as overtraining marker. Catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine) from adrenal medulla — fight-or-flight, ↑HR, ↑BP, glycogenolysis.

Key facts to memorize
  • Cortisol = glucocorticoid; circadian peak ~AM
  • Acute exercise cortisol rise is normal and adaptive
  • Chronic elevation = problem (overtraining, poor sleep, undereating)
  • T:C ratio drop ≥30% suggests overtraining
  • Epinephrine peaks during high-intensity work
Connection to your training

Your HRV trend (118→187 ms) is a downstream marker of autonomic balance — high HRV = parasympathetic dominance = lower catabolic load. Sleep debt and heat (33°C cost more recovery than 4×8s) push the other direction.

1 month backExercise TechniqueCh 15 · pp. 385-400

Resistance technique — bench & overhead press

Bench: 5 points of contact (head, upper back, glutes on bench; both feet on floor). Grip ~1.5× shoulder width. Bar path: descend to lower chest/sternum, slight curve up to over upper chest at lockout. Scaps retracted/depressed. Spotter at head. Overhead press: bar at clavicle, elbows slightly in front, brace, press up while staying tight, slight forward shift of head as bar passes face. Avoid lumbar hyperextension.

Key facts to memorize
  • Bench: 5 points of contact
  • Scaps retracted and depressed
  • Bar to lower chest, NOT throat
  • OHP: brace HARD; lumbar neutral
  • Push-press uses leg drive to start
Connection to your training

OHP is technique-heavy and underused — but for shoulder durability across a long training career it's worth keeping in.

Why is overhead press more demanding on lumbar stability than bench press?
How sure are you?
Answer
The load is overhead, creating extension torque at the lumbar spine. Without active bracing, the lifter compensates with lumbar hyperextension.
